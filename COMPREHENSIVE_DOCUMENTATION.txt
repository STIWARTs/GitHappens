EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
================================================================================

This document provides an exhaustive technical analysis of a state-of-the-art
machine learning solution for the Flipkart Gridlock 2.0 Hackathon. The project
predicts normalized traffic demand (0-1 scale) across ~1,190 geohash locations
in 15-minute intervals using a sophisticated ensemble of three gradient-boosted
tree models with extensive feature engineering and hyperparameter optimization.

Final Achievement:
  - Validation R² Score: 99.956%
  - RMSE: 0.0022
  - Ensemble: LightGBM (35.5%) + XGBoost (32.4%) + CatBoost (32.1%)


SECTION 1: PROBLEM STATEMENT & DATASET CHARACTERISTICS
================================================================================

1.1 CORE PROBLEM
----------------
Predict normalized traffic demand (continuous value in range [0, 1]) for 41,778
test rows. Each row represents a 15-minute time slot at a specific geohash
location. Test data spans Day 49 from 2:15 (0145 in 24h) to 13:45.

Evaluation Metric:
  score = max(0, 100 * R²(actual, predicted))
  where R² is the coefficient of determination measuring variance explained

1.2 TRAINING DATA
-----------------
Total: 77,299 rows across two days

Composition:
  - Day 48: ALL 96 timestamps (0:00 to 23:45, every 15 minutes) = 75,926 rows
  - Day 49: First 9 timestamps (0:00 to 2:00) = 1,373 rows
  
For Day 48, data was collected across ~1,190 unique geohash locations.

1.3 TEST DATA
-------------
41,778 rows representing:
  - Time range: Day 49, 2:15 to 13:45 (47 timestamps)
  - Approximately 889 geohash locations per time slot
  - Forward-looking prediction (predicting future demand)

1.4 CRITICAL EXPLORATORY DATA ANALYSIS FINDINGS
-----------------------------------------------

These findings fundamentally shaped all downstream architectural decisions:

Finding 1: GEOHASH DOMINANCE (79.9% variance explained)
  - Between-geohash variance explained: 79.9% of total variance
  - Within-geohash variance: 0.004 (extremely low)
  - Lag-1 autocorrelation within geohash: 0.9594 (extremely strong)
  
  IMPLICATION: Location identity (geohash) is the single most important
  predictor. Each location has an inherent "busyness" level that demand
  modulates around. Time-of-day acts as a multiplier on this baseline.

Finding 2: SYNTHETIC NOISE IN STATIC FEATURES
  Static features (RoadType, NumberofLanes, LargeVehicles, Landmarks) show
  INCONSISTENCY across rows for the SAME geohash:
  
    RoadType:         255 / 1,249 geohashes (20% inconsistent)
    NumberofLanes:  1,204 / 1,249 geohashes (96% inconsistent)
    LargeVehicles:  1,183 / 1,249 geohashes (95% inconsistent)
    Landmarks:      1,182 / 1,249 geohashes (95% inconsistent)
  
  IMPLICATION: These features are synthetically generated with deliberate noise
  (confirmed by Grab AI 2019 challenge provenance analysis). Models must learn
  the MODAL (majority) value per geohash while discounting per-row noise.

Finding 3: TEMPERATURE & WEATHER ARE DISTRACTORS
  - Temperature and Weather are perfectly correlated
  - Weather is essentially binned Temperature:
    Snowy:      Mean=3.59C  (Std=3.00, Range<=7.0)
    Rainy:      Mean=10.93C (Std=1.97, Range<=14.0)
    Foggy:      Mean=16.49C (Std=1.43, Range~14-19)
    Sunny:      Mean=24.02C (Std=4.03, Range>=19)
  - Despite deterministic relationship, Weather has ZERO impact on demand
  - All weather categories show mean demand ~0.092 (identical)
  
  IMPLICATION: Temperature/Weather should be deemphasized. Include for
  completeness but expect models to learn near-zero weights.

Finding 4: ROADTYPE & LANES HAVE MODERATE IMPACT (through geohash interaction)
  - Highway locations: Mean demand = 0.616 (vs global 0.092)
  - Street locations:  Mean demand = 0.273
  - Residential:       Mean demand = 0.057
  
  - 4-lane capacity: Mean demand = 0.606
  - 5-lane capacity: Mean demand = 0.611
  - 1-lane capacity: Mean demand = 0.087
  
  IMPLICATION: RoadType and NumberofLanes matter primarily through their
  correlation with specific geohashes. High-capacity infrastructure tends to
  be in high-demand locations. Models should learn MODAL road type per
  geohash rather than row-level noisy values.

Finding 5: STRONG TEMPORAL DIURNAL CYCLE
  Hourly demand pattern (Day 48 average):
    Hour 0:   0.057  (absolute trough, midnight)
    Hour 5:   0.104  (early morning rise)
    Hour 11:  0.117  (peak demand)
    Hour 14:  0.107  (afternoon plateau)
    Hour 18:  0.049  (evening trough)
    Hour 23:  0.093  (night recovery)
  
  Pattern: Bimodal - demand rises 0->11h, dips through afternoon/evening,
  troughs at ~18h, rises again overnight.
  
  Test window (2:15 to 13:45) captures the ENTIRE rising phase, meaning
  predictions require capturing this momentum.
  
  IMPLICATION: Temporal features MUST include cyclical encoding (sine/cosine)
  to capture the continuous, periodic nature of demand patterns.

Finding 6: SPATIAL CLUSTERING BY GEOHASH PREFIX
  Geohash data is heavily clustered by 4-character prefix:
    qp09: 41,391 rows (53.5% of data)
    qp03: 23,835 rows (30.8%)
    qp0d:  6,175 rows (8.0%)
    qp06:  2,511 rows (0.3%)
    qp08:  1,953 rows (0.3%)
    qp02:  1,434 rows (0.2%)
  
  IMPLICATION: Geographic clustering suggests that spatial prefix features
  (prefix4, prefix5 aggregates) can serve as effective fallback mechanisms
  for unseen geohashes in the test set. 10 test geohashes are unseen in
  training - spatial rollup features provide graceful degradation.


SECTION 2: COMPREHENSIVE FEATURE ENGINEERING
================================================================================

70+ features were engineered across 8 categories, carefully designed to capture
spatial, temporal, infrastructure, and historical patterns.

2.1 TEMPORAL FEATURES (8 features)
----------------------------------

Category 1A: Time Representation
  - hour: Integer hour from timestamp (0-23)
  - minute: Integer minute from timestamp (0-59)
  - time_slot: 15-minute interval index (0-95), derived as: h * 4 + m // 15
  
  Raw time values provide direct indexing into hourly/slot aggregates.

Category 1B: Cyclical Time Encoding (CRITICAL)
  - sin_hour: sin(2π * hour / 24)
  - cos_hour: cos(2π * hour / 24)
  - sin_slot: sin(2π * slot / 96)
  - cos_slot: cos(2π * slot / 96)
  
  RATIONALE: Time is inherently cyclic. 23:45 should be similar to 0:00 in
  terms of demand patterns. Linear encoding (e.g., hour=23) would place 23
  far from 0 in feature space. Sine/cosine encoding wraps the circle,
  preserving temporal periodicity. This is crucial for capturing the
  continuous nature of diurnal demand cycles.

Category 1C: Binary Time Indicators
  - is_morning_rush: 1 if 7 <= hour <= 10 else 0
  - is_evening_low: 1 if 16 <= hour <= 20 else 0
  - is_peak_demand: 1 if 9 <= hour <= 13 else 0
  - is_night: 1 if 0 <= hour <= 5 else 0
  
  RATIONALE: Binary flags enable the model to learn sharp demand shifts
  at period boundaries without needing to infer them from cyclical patterns.


2.2 GEOHASH IDENTITY FEATURES (10 features)
--------------------------------------------

These features capture the inherent demand baseline of each location.

Category 2A: Per-Geohash Aggregates (computed from Day 48)
  - gh_mean: Mean demand across all Day 48 samples for this geohash
  - gh_median: Median demand
  - gh_std: Standard deviation (spread around the mean)
  - gh_min: Minimum demand observed
  - gh_max: Maximum demand observed
  - gh_count: Number of samples for this geohash
  - gh_q25: 25th percentile (lower quartile)
  - gh_q75: 75th percentile (upper quartile)
  - gh_iqr: Interquartile range (q75 - q25)
  - gh_cv: Coefficient of variation (std / mean) - normalized spread
  
  RATIONALE: These statistics directly encode the "character" of each
  location. High gh_mean indicates a naturally congested area. High gh_std
  or gh_cv indicates that demand at this location is volatile. Low values
  suggest a naturally free-flowing area. The model learns to use these
  statistics as location-specific baselines.


2.3 GEOHASH × TIME INTERACTION FEATURES (9 features)
-----------------------------------------------------

These capture how demand varies by time within each geohash, providing
fine-grained temporal sensitivity per location.

Category 3A: Per-(Geohash, Hour) Statistics
  - gh_hour_mean: Average demand for this geohash at this hour
  - gh_hour_median: Median demand
  - gh_hour_std: Variability at this geohash-hour combination
  - gh_hour_min: Minimum observed
  - gh_hour_max: Maximum observed
  
  RATIONALE: Hour-specific stats reveal how demand evolves within each
  location's 24-hour cycle. E.g., "downtown district (qp09xxx) from 8-10am"
  has very different demand than "residential area (qp08xxx) from 2-4am".

Category 3B: Per-(Geohash, Time_slot) Statistics
  - gh_slot_mean: Mean demand for geohash at this specific 15-minute slot
  - gh_slot_median: Median demand
  - gh_slot_last: Last observed value from Day 48 at this geohash-slot combo
  
  RATIONALE: 15-minute granularity is finest grain lookup, capturing exact
  time-of-day patterns. If a geohash has extreme demand at slot 45 (11:00)
  in Day 48, gh_slot_mean will directly reflect this.

Category 3C: Day-over-day Continuity
  - gh_d48_same_slot: Direct lookup of demand from Day 48 at same slot
  
  RATIONALE: Due to high lag-1 autocorrelation (0.9594), demand in slot T
  is extremely similar to demand in slot T-1. Assuming Day 49 resembles
  Day 48 (same day-of-week seasonality), the exact same time slot in Day 48
  is a strong proxy for Day 49. This is the single strongest feature.


2.4 LAGGED & AUTOREGRESSIVE FEATURES (14 features)
---------------------------------------------------

These capture short-term momentum and immediate history.

Category 4A: Static Proxy Lags (using Day 48)
  - demand_lag_1_static: Demand at geohash-slot(T-1) from Day 48
  - demand_lag_2_static: Demand at geohash-slot(T-2) from Day 48
  - demand_lag_3_static: Demand at geohash-slot(T-3) from Day 48
  - demand_lag_4_static: Demand at geohash-slot(T-4) from Day 48
  
  Represents: 15, 30, 45, 60 minutes prior in the previous day

Category 4B: Rolling Window Features (static proxy)
  - demand_rolling_mean_2_static: Average of lag_1 and lag_2
  - demand_rolling_mean_4_static: Average of all four lags
  
  RATIONALE: Rolling means smooth out per-slot noise and capture broader
  15-45 minute trends. If demand is rising (lag_1 < lag_2 < lag_3),
  the rolling mean stays elevated. If demand is collapsing, rolling mean
  falls sharply.

Category 4C: Differencing (Rate of Change)
  - diff_1: lag_1_static minus current (lag_0, which is gh_d48_same_slot)
  
  RATIONALE: Captures momentum. Positive diff_1 means demand is declining
  (traffic clearing). Negative means demand is rising (congestion building).
  Models can learn that rising demand often continues rising (positive
  autocorrelation of the DERIVATIVE).

Category 4D: History-based Fallback Lags
  For Day 49 test data, where available:
  - Use actual Day 49 history (0:00-2:00) instead of Day 48 proxies
  - Fallback to static proxy if no Day 49 history exists for this slot
  
  RATIONALE: If we're predicting Day 49 slot 12 (3:00 AM) and we already
  know Day 49 slot 8 (2:00 AM) demand, use that ACTUAL value instead of
  Day 48's historical guess. This adds real information.


2.5 SPATIAL FEATURES (8 features)
---------------------------------

These encode geographic clustering and regional demand patterns.

Category 5A: Geohash Prefix Extraction
  - gh_prefix5: First 5 characters of geohash
  - gh_prefix4: First 4 characters
  - gh_prefix3: First 3 characters
  
  RATIONALE: Geohash prefixes represent increasingly broad geographic regions.
  prefix5 is ~1-2km radius. prefix4 is ~5-10km. prefix3 is ~50km+.
  These are used to compute regional aggregates for fallback when exact
  geohash has no training data.

Category 5B: Regional Aggregate Statistics
  For prefix5 regions:
  - prefix5_mean: Average demand in this 5-char region
  - prefix5_median: Median demand
  - prefix5_std: Demand variance within region
  - prefix5_count: Number of samples in region
  
  For prefix4 regions:
  - prefix4_mean: Average demand in this 4-char region
  - prefix4_median: Median demand
  
  RATIONALE: If a geohash is unseen in training, regional statistics provide
  graceful degradation. 10 test geohashes are unseen; these features ensure
  reasonable predictions for them.

Category 5C: Spatial Rank & Ratio
  - spatial_rank_ratio: gh_mean / prefix5_mean
  
  RATIONALE: Some geohashes are "hubs" (high ratio, ~1.5-2x regional avg).
  Others are "quiet zones" (low ratio, ~0.3-0.5x regional avg). This ratio
  expresses relative importance within the region.

Category 5D: Decoded Geographic Coordinates
  - latitude: Decoded latitude from geohash center
  - longitude: Decoded longitude from geohash center
  
  RATIONALE: Tree models can learn spatial patterns from raw coordinates,
  capturing geographic gradients not captured by discrete prefix bucketing.


2.6 ROAD INFRASTRUCTURE FEATURES (10 features)
-----------------------------------------------

These encode static road and vehicle data (with modal denoising).

Category 6A: Row-level Infrastructure (noisy, as-is from data)
  - road_type_encoded: Encoded as {Residential:0, Street:1, Highway:2}
  - is_highway: Binary flag (1 if Highway, 0 else)
  - is_street: Binary flag (1 if Street, 0 else)
  - is_high_capacity: Binary flag (1 if NumberofLanes >= 4, 0 else)
  - large_vehicles_encoded: Binary (1 if LargeVehicles=='Allowed')
  - landmarks_encoded: Binary (1 if Landmarks=='Yes')
  
  RATIONALE: Include these as-is because even with noise, they contain
  signal. Models can learn to discount the noise via regularization.

Category 6B: Modal (Denoised) Infrastructure per Geohash
  For each geohash, compute the MAJORITY VALUE across all training rows:
  - modal_road_type: Encoded majority road type
  - modal_lanes: Majority NumberofLanes value
  - modal_large_vehicles: Majority LargeVehicles flag
  - modal_landmarks: Majority Landmarks flag
  - modal_is_highway: Derived from modal_road_type
  - modal_is_high_capacity: Derived from modal_lanes
  
  RATIONALE: Since 95%+ of static features are inconsistent per geohash
  (synthetic noise), the modal value represents the "true" infrastructure
  for that location. Includes both row-level noisy AND geohash-level
  denoised versions, allowing the model to learn which to trust.


2.7 WEATHER FEATURES (2 features)
---------------------------------

Category 7A: Temperature
  - temperature_filled: Temperature with NaN imputed by weather-group mean,
    then backfilled with global median. Converted to float.
  
  RATIONALE: Temperature is deterministic (perfectly correlated with Weather)
  and has near-zero impact on demand in this dataset. However, it's included
  for completeness and to let the model learn that it's uninformative.

Category 7B: Weather Category
  - weather_encoded: Ordinal encoding {Snowy:0, Rainy:1, Foggy:2, Sunny:3}
  
  RATIONALE: Ordinal encoding preserves the implicit ordering (cold to hot)
  while keeping cardinality low for tree models.


2.8 INTERACTION & DERIVED FEATURES (6 features)
-----------------------------------------------

These combine multiple base features to capture multiplicative relationships.

Category 8A: Temporal × Demand Interactions
  - gh_mean_x_hour: gh_mean * sin_hour
  
  RATIONALE: Models demand modulation by time. A high-demand location
  (gh_mean) at peak hours (sin_hour > 0) should have even higher demand
  than either factor alone.

Category 8B: Infrastructure × Demand Interactions
  - modal_road_x_demand: modal_road_type * gh_mean
  - lanes_x_demand: modal_lanes * gh_mean
  
  RATIONALE: Highways with high baseline demand amplify each other.
  Tree models can learn these interactions directly, but explicit
  interaction features provide a head start and improved interpretability.

Category 8C: Demand Deviation from Expectations
  - demand_deviation_from_gh_mean: gh_d48_same_slot - gh_mean
  
    Shows how much the current slot deviates from the location's average.
    High positive = congestion spike. Negative = unusual quietness.
  
  - demand_deviation_from_global: gh_d48_same_slot - global_mean_demand
  
    Shows how much this geohash-slot combo deviates from global mean.
  
  - demand_percentile_in_gh: (gh_d48_same_slot - gh_min) / (gh_max - gh_min)
  
    Expresses current demand as a percentile within this geohash's range.
    Useful for understanding extreme vs typical values.


SECTION 3: MODEL ARCHITECTURE
================================================================================

3.1 ENSEMBLE PHILOSOPHY
-----------------------
A single model has inherent biases and blind spots. Three diverse gradient-
boosted tree implementations capture complementary patterns:

  LightGBM: Histogram-based splits, handles high-cardinality features, fast
  XGBoost: Exact greedy splits, different regularization philosophy
  CatBoost: Ordered boosting, strong with categorical variables

Individual models achieved ~99.93% R². Ensemble achieved 99.956% via weighted
blending, leveraging diversity to push performance beyond any single model.

3.2 MODEL 1: LIGHTGBM (Primary Model, 35.5% weight)
----------------------------------------------------

ARCHITECTURE:
  Framework: LightGBM (Microsoft's Light Gradient Boosting Machine)
  Boosting Type: GBDT (Gradient Boosting Decision Trees)
  Base Learner: Decision trees split on histogram-binned features
  Training Objective: RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error) regression
  Early Stopping: After 50 consecutive validation rounds without improvement

KEY HYPERPARAMETERS (from Optuna tuning, 40 trials):
  - n_estimators: Number of boosting iterations (trees to build)
    Range searched: 500-3000
  
  - learning_rate: Shrinkage for each tree's contribution
    Range searched: 0.005-0.1 (log scale)
    Lower values = slower learning but better generalization
  
  - max_depth: Maximum tree depth (0 = unlimited)
    Range searched: 4-12
    Deeper trees capture more complex interactions, risk overfitting
  
  - num_leaves: Maximum leaves per tree
    Range searched: 20-200
    Controls complexity per tree vs max_depth
  
  - min_child_samples: Minimum samples in leaf node
    Range searched: 10-100
    Higher = more conservative (less splits), reduces overfitting
  
  - subsample: Row subsampling fraction per tree (0.6-1.0)
    Fraction of rows used to build each tree. <1.0 adds randomness,
    reducing overfitting via bootstrap diversity.
  
  - colsample_bytree: Feature subsampling fraction (0.5-1.0)
    Fraction of features used per tree. <1.0 forces diversity.
  
  - reg_alpha: L1 regularization (leaf-weight penalty)
    Range: 1e-8 to 10.0 (log scale)
    Encourages sparse trees with fewer non-zero leaf weights
  
  - reg_lambda: L2 regularization (squared leaf-weight penalty)
    Range: 1e-8 to 10.0 (log scale)
    Smoother regularization than L1
  
  - min_split_gain: Minimum loss reduction for split
    Range: 1e-8 to 1.0 (log scale)
    Only split if it reduces loss by at least this much

OPTIMIZATION PROCESS:
  1. Optuna sampler: TPE (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator)
  2. Number of trials: 40
  3. Objective: Maximize R² score on validation set
  4. Metric computation: R² = 100 * (1 - SS_res / SS_tot)
  5. Predictions clipped to [0, 1] after each trial

FINAL PERFORMANCE:
  - Validation R²: 99.9516%
  - RMSE: Extremely low (capturing ~99.95% of variance)


3.3 MODEL 2: XGBOOST (Secondary Model, 32.4% weight)
-----------------------------------------------------

ARCHITECTURE:
  Framework: XGBoost (Extreme Gradient Boosting)
  Boosting Type: GBDT with exact greedy split finding
  Base Learner: Decision trees with different growth strategy than LightGBM
  Training Objective: reg:squarederror (squared error for regression)
  Early Stopping: After 50 validation rounds without improvement

KEY HYPERPARAMETERS (from Optuna tuning, 30 trials):
  - n_estimators: 500-3000
  
  - learning_rate: 0.005-0.1 (log scale)
  
  - max_depth: 4-12
  
  - min_child_weight: Minimum sum of hessian weights in child
    Range: 1-50
    Higher = more conservative. Analogous to min_child_samples in LightGBM
  
  - subsample: 0.6-1.0
  
  - colsample_bytree: 0.5-1.0
  
  - reg_alpha: 1e-8 to 10.0 (log scale)
  
  - reg_lambda: 1e-8 to 10.0 (log scale)
  
  - gamma: Minimum loss reduction for split
    Range: 1e-8 to 5.0 (log scale)
    Only split if it reduces loss by gamma. High gamma = fewer splits.

TRAINING PROCESS:
  1. Optuna TPE sampler with 30 trials
  2. Maximize R² on validation set
  3. Clip predictions to [0, 1]
  4. Early stopping after 50 rounds

FINAL PERFORMANCE:
  - Validation R²: 99.9335%
  - Performance slightly below LightGBM but provides ensemble diversity


3.4 MODEL 3: CATBOOST (Tertiary Model, 32.1% weight)
-----------------------------------------------------

ARCHITECTURE:
  Framework: CatBoost (Categorical Boosting)
  Boosting Type: Ordered boosting with categorical feature support
  Base Learner: Oblivious trees (same split feature at same level)
  Training Objective: RMSE regression
  Early Stopping: After 50 validation rounds

DISTINGUISHING FEATURE:
  - Ordered Boosting: Reduces overfitting by using different data
    permutations to compute leaf predictions vs tree structure
  - Native Categorical Support: Automatically handles categorical features
    without one-hot encoding (though our pipeline still encodes them)

KEY HYPERPARAMETERS (from Optuna tuning, 25 trials):
  - iterations: Number of boosting iterations (500-3000)
  
  - learning_rate: 0.005-0.1 (log scale)
  
  - depth: Tree depth (4-10, shallower than other models)
  
  - l2_leaf_reg: L2 regularization coefficient
    Range: 0.1-10.0 (log scale)
    Higher = more regularization
  
  - border_count: Number of bins for continuous feature quantization
    Range: 32-255
    Higher = finer granularity, slower training
  
  - bagging_temperature: Temperature for bootstrap distribution
    Range: 0.0-10.0
    Higher = more randomness in bootstrap samples
  
  - random_strength: Amount of random noise in splits
    Range: 1e-8 to 10.0 (log scale)
    Encourages diversity without explicit subsampling

FINAL PERFORMANCE:
  - Validation R²: 99.9311%
  - Consistent with other models


3.5 ENSEMBLE WEIGHTING STRATEGY
-------------------------------

Rather than simple equal averaging, weights are computed from validation R²:

  1. For each model, extract validation R² score
  2. Apply softmax-like weighting (temperature = 5 for smooth scaling):
       weight_i = exp(5 * (R²_i - max_R²)) / sum(exp(5 * (R²_j - max_R²)))
  
  3. Resulting weights approximate softmax but preserve relative differences
  
  Final weights:
    LightGBM: 0.355 (highest R²)
    XGBoost: 0.324
    CatBoost: 0.321

  Final Ensemble Prediction = 0.355 * LGBM_pred + 0.324 * XGB_pred + 0.321 * CB_pred

PERFORMANCE OF ENSEMBLE:
  - Validation R²: 99.9560% (BEST)
  - RMSE: 0.0022
  - Ensemble outperforms all individual models, proving diversity benefit


SECTION 4: TRAINING STRATEGY & VALIDATION DESIGN
================================================================================

4.1 TEMPORAL VALIDATION SPLIT (CRITICAL for time-series)
---------------------------------------------------------

A standard random train/test split would cause INFORMATION LEAKAGE because
future data could influence past predictions. Instead, a time-based split
was used:

  Training Split: Day 48, timestamps 0:00 to 17:45 (slots 0-71)
  Validation Split: Day 48, timestamps 18:00 to 23:45 (slots 72-95)
  
  This respects temporal ordering: models train on early day, validate on
  late day, simulating forward-looking prediction.

4.2 FEATURE FITTING
-------------------

All aggregate statistics (per-geohash, per-hour, regional) are computed
ONLY from the Day 48 training split (slots 0-71), preventing feature
leakage from validation data.

  1. FeatureEngine.fit(Day48_train_only)
     - Computes geohash_stats, geohash_hour_stats, prefix5/4 stats, etc.
  
  2. FeatureEngine.transform(Day48_train + Day48_val + Day49 + Test)
     - Applies fitted statistics to all datasets
  
  This ensures validation and test predictions use only information available
  at prediction time (Day 48 0:00-17:45).

4.3 HYPERPARAMETER OPTIMIZATION (OPTUNA)
-----------------------------------------

For each model independently:
  
  1. Optuna Study created with TPE (Tree-structured Parzen Estimator) sampler
  2. For each trial:
     a. Sample hyperparameters from defined ranges
     b. Train model on Day48_train with parameters
     c. Evaluate on Day48_val
     d. Clip predictions to [0, 1]
     e. Compute R² score
  
  3. Maximize R² over all trials
  
  Results:
    LightGBM: 40 trials, best R² = 99.9516%
    XGBoost: 30 trials, best R² = 99.9335%
    CatBoost: 25 trials, best R² = 99.9311%

4.4 FINAL FULL RETRAIN (BEST PRACTICE)
---------------------------------------

After determining optimal hyperparameters and ensemble weights, ALL models
were retrained on the COMPLETE training set (100% of Day 48 + Day 49 0:00-2:00):

  1. Use best hyperparameters from Optuna tuning
  2. Train on ALL 77,299 training rows (no validation split)
  3. No early stopping (train to completion)
  4. Use these fully-trained models for test predictions
  
  RATIONALE: Validation set was only needed for hyperparameter selection.
  Once hyperparameters are fixed, throwing away training data hurts
  performance. Retraining on full data maximizes learning capacity.

SECTION 6: PIPELINE ORCHESTRATION
================================================================================

The main execution script run_pipeline.py follows this sequence:

STEP 1: DATA LOADING
  - load_data() reads train.csv and test.csv
  - Separates Day 48 (full) and Day 49 train (0:00-2:00) subsets
  - Prints shape summaries
  
  Output:
    train.csv: 77,299 rows (Day 48 + Day 49 0:00-2:00)
    test.csv: 41,778 rows (Day 49 2:15-13:45)

STEP 2: FEATURE ENGINEERING
  - Instantiate FeatureEngine()
  - fe.fit(train_raw) computes all aggregate statistics from Day 48
  - fe.transform(all datasets) applies 70+ feature transformations
  
  Output:
    - train_featured with 70+ new columns
    - test_featured with 70+ new columns

STEP 3: LAG FEATURES (Static Proxy)
  - build_lag_features_static() adds demand_lag_1/2/3/4_static
  - Fallback to Day 48 demand at (geohash, slot - lag) for test data
  
  Output:
    - Lagged features for train and test

STEP 4: LAG FEATURES (History-based)
  - build_lag_features_from_history() uses actual Day 49 history when available
  - For Day 49 0:00-2:00, use actual measured demand instead of Day 48 proxy
  - Fallback to static proxy for slots before 0:00 (wraparound)
  
  Output:
    - History lags for test data, NaN-filled with static proxies

STEP 5: TEMPORAL VALIDATION SPLIT
  - temporal_train_val_split(train_featured, val_hours=6)
  - Day 48 slots 0-71 (0:00-17:45) -> X_train, y_train
  - Day 48 slots 72-95 (18:00-23:45) -> X_val, y_val
  
  Output:
    - X_train: 45,556 rows x 71 features
    - y_train: 45,556 demand targets
    - X_val: 13,392 rows x 71 features
    - y_val: 13,392 demand targets
    - Day 49 0:00-2:00 reserved for potential history lag computation

STEP 6: MODEL TRAINING WITH OPTUNA
  - ModelTrainer.train_lightgbm(X_train, y_train, X_val, y_val, n_trials=40)
  - ModelTrainer.train_xgboost(X_train, y_train, X_val, y_val, n_trials=30)
  - ModelTrainer.train_catboost(X_train, y_train, X_val, y_val, n_trials=25)
  
  For each trial:
    a. Sample hyperparameters from Optuna ranges
    b. Train model
    c. Evaluate R² on validation set
    d. Record best hyperparameters
  
  Output:
    - Best hyperparameters for each model
    - Validation R² scores (99.95%+ for each)

STEP 7: ENSEMBLE WEIGHTING
  - compute_ensemble_weights() from validation R² scores
  - Softmax weighting: weight_i = exp(5*(R²_i - max)) / Z
  
  Output:
    - LightGBM: 0.355
    - XGBoost: 0.324
    - CatBoost: 0.321

STEP 8: FULL RETRAINING
  - Retrain all three models on complete training data (no val split)
  - Use best hyperparameters from Optuna
  - Train to full convergence
  
  Output:
    - Model objects ready for predictions

STEP 9: TEST PREDICTIONS
  - Predict on test_featured using all three models
  - Clip predictions to [0, 1] range
  - Ensemble: pred_ensemble = 0.355*lgbm + 0.324*xgb + 0.321*cb
  
  Output:
    - 41,778 predictions in [0, 1]

STEP 10: SUBMISSION GENERATION
  - Create submission dataframe with columns [Index, demand]
  - Write to CSV in required format
  - Validate submission (check completeness, bounds, data types)
  
  Output:
    - submission_ensemble.csv


SECTION 7: HANDLING EDGE CASES & ROBUSTNESS
================================================================================

7.1 UNSEEN GEOHASHES (10 in test set)
-------------------------------------
Problem: 10 test geohashes have no training data

Solution 1 - Direct Fallback:
  If geohash not in geohash_stats, use global_mean_demand (0.092)

Solution 2 - Spatial Prefix Fallback (Preferred):
  1. Try to get statistics from prefix5 (5-char geohash prefix)
  2. If prefix5 also unseen, try prefix4 (4-char prefix)
  3. If still missing, use global mean
  
  Example flow:
    qp0abc7 -> prefix5=qp0ab -> mean=0.103 (from prefix5 stats)
    If qp0ab also unseen -> prefix4=qp0a -> mean=0.098
    If qp0a also unseen -> global=0.092

Result: Graceful degradation from fine-grained to coarse-grained spatial
statistics, preventing model collapse for unseen locations.

7.2 MISSING TEMPERATURE & WEATHER
---------------------------------
Problem: Some rows have NaN in Temperature or Weather

Solution:
  - For Temperature: Fill with weather-group mean (e.g., all Rainy rows
    get avg Rainy temperature). Remaining NaN filled with global median.
  
  - For Weather: Required columns, checked during data loading. If missing,
    raise error (data integrity check).

7.3 EXTREME VALUES & CLIPPING
-----------------------------
All model predictions clipped to [0, 1] range:
  pred_clipped = np.clip(pred, 0, 1)

Rationale: Predictions outside [0, 1] violate problem definition (normalized
demand). Clipping prevents invalid submissions. Tree models rarely predict
outside training range, but clipping provides safety margin.

7.4 NUMERICAL STABILITY
-----------------------
Division-by-zero safeguards in feature engineering:
  - Coefficient of variation: cv = std / (mean + 1e-10)
  - Spatial rank: ratio = gh_mean / (prefix5_mean + 1e-10)
  - Percentile: (value - min) / (max - min + 1e-10)

Adding small epsilon (1e-10) prevents division by zero without materially
affecting calculations.

SECTION 9: KEY LEARNINGS & DESIGN DECISIONS
================================================================================

9.1 WHY GEOHASH IDENTITY DOMINATES
----------------------------------
Geohash explains 79.9% of variance because urban demand is primarily supply-
constrained. High-demand locations (highways, commercial districts) have
inherently higher congestion. Low-demand areas (residential, rural) stay
quiet. Time-of-day creates modest multiplicative effects but doesn't change
the fundamental ranking of locations.

This insight immediately suggests:
  1. Per-geohash baselines are critical features
  2. Interaction features (geohash × time) are more valuable than pure time
  3. Weather and other global factors matter less

9.2 WHY MODAL FEATURES WORK
---------------------------
95%+ inconsistency in static features means raw per-row values are noise.
Computing modal (majority) values per geohash provides denoised estimates
of true infrastructure. Including BOTH noisy and denoised versions allows
models to learn which signal is reliable.

9.3 WHY CYCLICAL ENCODING IS ESSENTIAL
--------------------------------------
Demand follows a 24-hour cycle. Linear time features (hour=0 vs hour=23)
treat midnight and 11pm as far apart, breaking the temporal periodicity.
Sine/cosine encoding preserves circularity, enabling the model to capture
that 23:45 is adjacent to 0:15 in the demand cycle.

9.4 WHY TEMPORAL VALIDATION SPLIT MATTERS
------------------------------------------
Random splits cause leakage in time series: validation data influences
training. Temporal splits (early days train, late days validate) properly
simulate prediction: does the model trained on morning predict afternoon?
This is more realistic and produces more honest performance estimates.

9.5 WHY ENSEMBLE OUTPERFORMS INDIVIDUALS
-----------------------------------------
Single models achieve 99.93% R². Ensemble achieves 99.956%. The improvement
comes from:
  1. Averaging reduces variance (individual model prediction noise cancels)
  2. Diversity: Each model has different biases and blind spots
  3. Weighted averaging (softmax of R²) gives higher weight to better models

In this case, all three models are nearly identical (within 0.02 percentage
points), making the gain modest but measurable.

9.6 WHY RETRAIN ON FULL DATA AFTER TUNING
------------------------------------------
Validation set is needed only for hyperparameter selection. Once optimal
hyperparameters are identified, training on the full dataset (including
validation set) maximizes the amount of data the model sees. This reduces
variance and improves generalization. Early stopping is disabled during
full retraining since we're not comparing against a validation set.
